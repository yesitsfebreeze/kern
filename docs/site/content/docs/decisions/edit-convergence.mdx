title: "Edit convergence"
---

**When two statements about the same thing conflict, kern supersedes — the new
claim retires the old through a status flip and a `Supersedes` edge — rather
than keeping Wikipedia-style parallel revisions; full versioning, edit
rationales, and revert rate-limits were evaluated against Wikipedia's actual
mechanics and deliberately deferred behind explicit trigger conditions.** The
hybrid that was adopted shipped: supersession with bi-temporal stamps, alternate
phrasings captured non-destructively as `Rephrase` edges, and LLM-adjudicated
contradiction on the background tick. The two smaller additions the note also
adopted — a `ReasonKind::Edit` rationale edge and a 3RR-style producer
rate-limit — were **not built**.

## Context

The question the note (`docs/kern/wikipedia-edit-convergence.md`) set out to
answer: when multiple producers repeatedly edit the same knowledge — the same
`external_id`, or the same proposition in different words — does kern need
Wikipedia's machinery to converge on a single stable statement instead of
oscillating or forking?

Wikipedia is the existence proof that a noisy, adversarial crowd can converge
on one article for contested topics, and it takes four *coupled* mechanisms to
do it: an append-only revision history (nothing is ever overwritten), the
three-revert rule (a hard bound on edit wars), talk pages (disagreement moves
to a slower channel where citations beat assertions), and reputation tiers
(trusted editors' changes stick longer). None of the four is sufficient alone.

## Alternatives considered

**Adopt the full model** — inline revision vectors, per-producer reputation,
rate-limited reverts, protected thoughts. The failure mode is not that it
would not work; it is that it solves a threat kern does not have. Wikipedia's
machinery exists because its editors are anonymous, unaligned, and sometimes
adversarial. kern's writers are mostly cooperating agents with shared goals.
Building four coupled mechanisms against a threat model with no observed
instances is pure speculative weight, and each mechanism is a maintenance
surface (reputation alone implies identity, scoring, and decay subsystems).

**Bare supersede with no history** — the pre-decision status quo. Fails on
three counts the note documents: no record of *why* a statement changed, no
way to answer "what did we believe last month", and no convergence story at
all when two producers ping-pong the same source — each re-ingest silently
wins until the next one.

**The adopted hybrid** keeps supersession as the default and takes only the
cheapest piece of each Wikipedia mechanism: the supersede *chain* stands in
for revision history, `Rephrase` edges and the reason graph stand in for the
talk page, and everything else waits for evidence it is needed.

## Why the hybrid wins

The observation that makes it cheap: **the supersede chain is already a
minimal history.** Superseding marks the old entity `Superseded`, points
`superseded_by` at the replacement, stamps both temporal clocks, and evicts
the old id from the vector indexes while leaving it in its kern
(`supersede`, `src/base/accept.rs:346`; the clock stamping is
`stamp_invalidated`, `src/base/types.rs:355`). Nothing is erased. The chain
is walkable backwards (`superseded_ancestors`, `src/base/reason.rs:17`) and
surfaced on demand through the query tool's `include_history` and `as_of`
options — append-only revisions in effect, without a second storage mechanism.
The full lifecycle is documented in
[Time & contradiction](../concepts/time.mdx).

Convergence between disagreeing producers is then a judgement call, and kern
places it deliberately: not at query time, not synchronously at ingest, but on
the background tick. A near-duplicate whose wording differs is first recorded
as a non-destructive `Rephrase` edge (`src/ingest/dedup.rs:23`); the tick
later asks the LLM whether the new statement is an update, a contradiction, or
merely related (`do_classify_contradiction`, `src/tick/tasks.rs:114`), and
only an unambiguous verdict triggers `supersede_by_contradiction`
(`src/base/accept.rs:470`).

Two guards shape the outcome, and both are the NPOV instinct translated into
code:

- **It fails open to coexistence.** `parse_contradiction`
  (`src/base/accept.rs:460`) returns `Related` for hedged, empty, or
  unparseable verdicts. A missed contradiction leaves two claims standing —
  recoverable, visible, resolvable later. A wrongful supersede silently hides
  a true claim behind a false one. When in doubt, kern keeps both points of
  view rather than electing a winner.
- **Kind gates the verdict.** Only a same-kind near-duplicate may supersede
  (`src/ingest/dedup.rs:39`) — a distilled `Claim` can never invalidate a
  user-asserted `Fact`. This is the one place the model *does* weight trust
  asymmetrically, and it is a two-level static rule, not a reputation system.

Worked example — the deadline moves. January: "deadline is March" is captured
from ticket `T-42`. February: the same ticket is re-captured saying April. The
`external_id` matches, so the explicit path fires with no LLM involved: the
April claim is inserted, the March claim becomes `Superseded`, its world-time
window is closed at April's `valid_from`, its transaction stamp records when
kern learned of it, and a `Supersedes` edge links the two. A query today
returns April only; `as_of` January returns March only; `include_history`
returns both, labelled. Had the second capture arrived without an external id —
same proposition, different words — it would instead have travelled the
`Rephrase` → tick → classifier route to the same four-step mutation.

<Callout type="info" title="Where the revert-war bound actually lives">
Wikipedia needs 3RR because a revert restores the old text and the war can
oscillate forever. kern's supersede is asymmetric — each step produces a
*new* entity and extends the chain — so an A/B ping-pong leaves a growing,
visible chain rather than an invisible oscillation. That makes the missing
rate-limit observable after the fact (a long chain on one `external_id`), but
nothing today bounds it while it happens.
</Callout>

## What shipped vs. what the note assumed

The note's deferrals held: no inline revision storage, no reputation tiers, no
protected thoughts — and none of the stated trigger conditions (chains past ~5
hops, producers ping-ponging a source, byte-level blame requests) has fired.

The note's two *adopted* additions diverged:

- **`ReasonKind::Edit` was not built.** The edge vocabulary
  (`src/base/types.rs:66`) has no `Edit` kind. Its role is partly covered by
  `Rephrase`, which shipped later and carries the alternate wording on the
  edge (`merge_duplicate`, `src/base/accept.rs:57`, idempotent under repeat) —
  but a wording is not a rationale. "Why does this thought now say X" is
  answerable only insofar as the new text implies it; no edit-summary
  free-text is recorded.
- **The 3RR-lite rate-limit was not built.** No per-producer supersede
  counting exists on the ingest path. Nothing marks a producer that
  supersedes the same `external_id` N times in a window.
- **Shipped beyond the note:** the bi-temporal clocks (`valid_from`/`valid_to`
  world time, `invalidated_at` transaction time) and the `as_of` /
  `include_history` query surface postdate the note and make the
  chain-as-history claim genuinely queryable rather than merely walkable.
  Superseded facts also lose their GC immunity and are spilled to the cold
  tier rather than held hot forever — see
  [Heat & compaction](../concepts/heat-and-compaction.mdx).

One honest caveat inherits from retention: the chain is a *minimal* history,
not an archival one. A superseded revision that goes cold is evicted to the
capped cold tier, and the temporal side-map does not follow it there
([Time & contradiction](../concepts/time.mdx#how-the-clocks-survive-a-restart)),
so deep history degrades gracefully rather than persisting byte-perfect. That
is consistent with the decision — kern is a memory, not an encyclopedia — but
anyone reaching for Wikipedia-grade auditability should know the difference.
