---
title: "Stigmergy over gardening"
---

**kern decides what to forget through use-marks — heat deposited by reads and by
the pulse, evaporating exponentially — rather than through curation, retention
policies, or scheduled cleanup rules.** The core of this decision shipped: every
entity carries a decaying `heat` scalar (`src/base/heat.rs:7`), retrieval and
the pulse both deposit into it, and an hourly collector evicts what has gone
cold (`run_gc`, `src/tick/stigmergy.rs`). The convergence metrics the original note
proposed — the instrument for proving the model works — were not built, and the
ranking-side unification it proposed did not happen. Both gaps are detailed
below.

## Context

A memory system that only accretes is eventually useless: recall degrades as
the candidate set grows, and the graph's structure stops meaning anything. So
something has to decide what to drop, and the question is *who writes that
policy*. The design note this page ports
(`docs/kern/stigmergy-self-improving.md`) started from an honest observation
about the pre-1.0 code: kern already had the *shape* of a trace-and-decay
system — an access count, a last-access timestamp, a tree-walking pulse — but
without the discipline of one. The decay rate was an untunable constant, two
redundant freshness signals interacted in non-obvious ways, and the pulse
walked the tree without reinforcing anything, pure overhead.

The decision was whether to formalise that machinery into an explicit
stigmergic model — one scalar, one deposit rule, one evaporation rate, one
floor — or to replace it with an administered retention policy.

## Alternatives considered

**Administered retention** — LRU lists, per-kern quotas, TTLs, "keep the last
N", or a human pruning by hand. Every variant requires someone to encode, in
advance, what will matter later. A TTL deletes the fact you needed on day 31; a
quota evicts by an ordering (insertion, size, age) that has no relationship to
usefulness; manual gardening does not scale past one attentive operator and
silently stops happening. The deeper failure is architectural: policy knobs
multiply. Each special case ("but keep facts", "but not this project") becomes
another rule, and the rules interact.

**Richer stigmergy variants** were also weighed and explicitly rejected in the
note, and remain unbuilt by choice:

- *Per-edge pheromone* (heat on reasons, not entities) — storage cost for a
  weak signal until the reason graph is much denser. Edges do carry a
  `traversal_count`, but it feeds edge scoring, not retention.
- *Multiple pheromone types* (food/danger in real ant colonies) — a single
  scalar suffices; entity kinds already provide orthogonal typing.
- *Continuous fluid models* — discrete decay matches the tick-based
  architecture.
- *Cross-peer heat gossip* — heat is deliberately local provenance. What a
  peer found useful is not evidence about what this replica will need, and a
  gossiped usage signal is an obvious injection surface.

## Why the trace wins

The stigmergic model has three knobs — deposit magnitude, evaporation rate,
threshold floor — and everything else is emergent. Retention becomes a
consequence of the system's own behaviour rather than a policy someone
maintains: what gets read stays; what the structure keeps reachable stays warm;
what neither happens to evaporates.

The shipped mechanics, in brief (the full treatment is in
[Stigmergy GC & gravitons](../concepts/stigmergy.mdx) and
[Heat, decay & self-compaction](../concepts/heat-and-compaction.mdx)):

- Reads deposit `1.0` heat via `stamp_access` (`src/retrieval/score.rs:247`),
  off the query hot path.
- The pulse re-deposits `0.5 × strength` on every entity it reaches walking
  down from the root (`src/tick/pulse.rs:113`), decaying geometrically per
  level (`PULSE_DECAY = 0.5`, floor `0.05`, `src/base/constants.rs:47`). This
  is the note's key structural addition: reachability from the root is itself a
  reinforcement signal, so an idle daemon still maintains its own spine.
- Decay is lazy — computed from the age of the last deposit, never on a timer
  (`heat::decayed`, `src/base/heat.rs:25`), with a **7-day half-life** by
  default (`src/base/heat.rs:18`). A deposit decays the stale value first,
  then adds (`src/base/heat.rs:41`), so a deposit never resurrects old
  magnitude.
- Once an hour, `run_gc` evicts entities that are cold (*aged* heat below
  `0.01`), stale (untouched for 7 days), and not durable
  (`is_cold_victim`, `src/tick/stigmergy.rs:27`) — spilling each victim to the
  cold tier before dropping it.

Worked through with the shipped constants: a claim ingested and read exactly
once starts at heat `1.0`. It falls below the `0.01` floor after
`log2(100) ≈ 6.6` half-lives — about **46 days** — and only then, being also
older than the 7-day staleness gate, becomes a GC victim. A claim the pulse
still reaches at strength `0.5` gets topped up every maintenance pass and never
qualifies. A claim read even once a week never decays past half its peak. The
system's hot set converges on what is actually used plus what is structurally
central, with nobody writing a policy that says so.

<Callout type="info" title="Durability is the one administered exception">
One rule is deliberately policy, not emergence: active `Fact` and `Document`
entities are immune to collection, and supersession revokes the immunity. The
stigmergic field governs everything the user has not explicitly marked as
load-bearing. See
[Heat & compaction](../concepts/heat-and-compaction.mdx#what-stops-it-deleting-something-you-still-needed)
for the exact guarantee.
</Callout>

## What shipped vs. what the note assumed

The note is point-in-time (it references the pre-1.0 `crates/` layout) and
several of its proposals landed differently or not at all.

**The qbst unification did not happen.** The note's central formula proposal —
replace the two-term ranking boost (`log` access count + exponential recency)
with the single heat scalar — is not what shipped. Heat became a *retention*
signal only. Ranking still uses the original two-term `qbst`
(`qbst`, `src/retrieval/score.rs:64`) with its own separate constants: access weight
`0.02`, recency weight `0.05`, recency half-life **24 hours**, cap `0.1`
(`src/base/constants.rs:10`). The note's complaint that the 24h half-life was
hard-coded is half-resolved — it is now configurable via
`qbst_recency_half_life_secs` (`src/config/retrieval.rs:32`) — but kern today
runs two freshness signals with different half-lives: a 24-hour one for
ranking, a 7-day one for retention. Defensible (ranking freshness and retention
are different questions) but it is a divergence the note argued against, and
the offline NDCG sweep that was supposed to tune either constant was never run.

**The half-life is 7 days, not the proposed 36 hours.** The note's §4.1
derivation argued for 1–2 days so that "a week of silence overrides a month of
heat". The shipped default errs heavily toward retention; with the GC floor at
`0.01` the effective memory of a single access is ~46 days, not ~1 week.

**The convergence metrics were not built.** The Gini coefficient, top-10
stability, and median-path-length criteria — the note's §5, the instrument for
answering "is the access distribution actually converging?" — do not exist.
`src/tick/stigmergy.rs` exists under the name the note reserved for the metrics
module, but contains the GC instead. Until something like them is built, the
claim that the corpus converges on efficient paths is a design intention, not a
measurement.

**Two things shipped that the note never asked for.** Spill-before-drop:
eviction writes the victim to a capped cold LMDB tier first and keeps it hot if
the write fails (`evict_victims`, `src/tick/stigmergy.rs`), so GC is a
demotion, not a deletion. And a federation carve-out: durable-kind immunity
holds only for local kerns — a peer cannot pin unreclaimable storage by
labelling its gossip `kind=Fact` (`src/tick/stigmergy.rs`).
