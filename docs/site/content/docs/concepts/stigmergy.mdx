kern decides what to forget from traces that reads leave behind, not from a
retention policy anyone wrote down. This page covers the trace itself — how it
is deposited, how it evaporates, and how collection follows from it — and then
gravitons: the plural, weighted attractors that bias which regions of the graph
stay dense and which results surface first.

## The metaphor, briefly

Ants do not hold a map. They deposit pheromone as they walk, other ants prefer
paths that already smell of pheromone, and the pheromone evaporates. Short paths
get retraversed sooner, so they accumulate faster than they evaporate; long
paths fade out. No ant decides anything about the route; the route is a
consequence of the medium. That is stigmergy: coordination through modification
of a shared environment rather than through direct signalling or a central plan.

That is the entire loan from biology. The rest of this page is the mechanism,
which is exponential decay and a threshold test, and owes nothing further to
insects. Why this was chosen over scheduled cleanup or manual curation — and
what was proposed and *not* built, notably the convergence metrics that would
substantiate the self-organisation claim — is in
[Stigmergy over gardening](../decisions/stigmergy-over-gardening.mdx).

## The trace a read leaves

Retrieval is not read-only in the bookkeeping sense. When a query returns an
entity, `stamp_access` (`src/retrieval/score.rs:217`) does three things to it:
increments a per-replica access counter, sets `accessed_at`, and deposits heat.

Where that stamping happens matters. The MCP query path takes only a read lock
while ranking, and pushes the access stamps onto the tick queue as a
`CommitAccess` task instead of writing them inline
(`src/mcp/tools_query.rs:223`, handled by `do_commit_access` at
`src/tick/tasks.rs:448`). The trace is deposited off the hot path — a recall
never blocks on a write, and the stamp deliberately avoids bumping the graph's
mutation epoch so it does not invalidate the query cache
(`src/retrieval/score.rs:236`).

The second depositor is the pulse. Every maintenance pass walks the kern tree
from the root and re-deposits heat on the entities it reaches, at half strength
per level, stopping below `PULSE_THRESHOLD = 0.05` (`src/tick/pulse.rs:108`).
This is the part with no biological counterpart and it is load-bearing: it means
the trace field is refreshed by *structural reachability from the root*, not
only by explicit reads. An idle daemon still lays pheromone on its own main
paths.

Evaporation is exponential with a 7-day half-life, computed lazily from the age
of the last deposit rather than decremented on a schedule. See [Heat, decay &
self-compaction](./heat-and-compaction.mdx#decay-is-lazy-and-age-based) for the
function and its consequences.

## From trace to collection

`run_gc` (`src/tick/stigmergy.rs:41`) runs per kern, once an hour
(`STIGMERGY_GC_INTERVAL`), gated on a clock sanity check that refuses to sweep
when the clock is unreadable or has gone backwards. It collects the entities for
which `is_cold_victim` (`src/tick/stigmergy.rs:13`) holds, which is the
conjunction of:

- **not durable** — `Fact` and `Document` are immune while active; supersession
  revokes that immunity;
- **cold** — heat, *aged before the comparison*, below `COLD_HEAT_THRESHOLD =
  0.01`;
- **stale** — last touch (`accessed_at`, falling back to `created_at`) older
  than `COLD_GC_AGE = 7 days`.

Every victim is written to the cold LMDB table before it is dropped from the hot
graph, and a failed write keeps it hot for the next pass (`evict_victims`,
`src/tick/stigmergy.rs:78`). The full guarantee, including where it stops being
absolute, is set out in [Heat, decay &
self-compaction](./heat-and-compaction.mdx#what-stops-it-deleting-something-you-still-needed).

What is worth naming here is the *shape* of the policy rather than its
constants. There is no LRU list, no per-kern quota, no age-ordered queue, and no
"keep the last N" rule. Selection is a linear scan over a kern's entities
applying a pure predicate over values the system was already maintaining for
ranking. Retention emerges from usage; it is not administered.

<Callout type="info" title="The known cost of that simplicity">
Victim selection is O(entities) per kern per sweep, and the cold tier is
brute-force cosine search with no index. Both are acceptable at current
scale, and both are tracked as open work in
[`ROADMAP.md`](https://github.com/yesitsfebreeze/kern/blob/master/docs/oracle/ROADMAP.md)
rather than quietly ignored.
</Callout>

## Gravitons

Heat says what has been used. Gravitons say what this kern is *for* — and unlike
heat, they are set deliberately.

A graviton is a named focus attractor stored as a child kern of the root: a name
(`graviton_text`), an embedding of its seed text (`graviton_vec`), and a scalar
`mass` defaulting to `1.0`. You manage them through the `graviton` MCP tool
(`tool_graviton`, `src/mcp/tools_admin.rs:89`) with `action=list|add|remove`;
seed text may be a short phrase or an entire document, embedded whole.

They act at two points.

**At ingest.** Routing picks the child whose graviton is nearest by *effective*
distance — `cosine_distance / max(mass, 1e-6)` (`effective_distance`,
`src/base/accept.rs:20`; used by `route_to_child_id` at
`src/base/accept.rs:732`). Mass is therefore a pull multiplier: two gravitons at
equal cosine distance are not tied if one is heavier. Content that matches no
graviton falls through to a catch-all `generic` child, which has an empty
graviton vector and so never wins on similarity.

**At query time.** After ranking, `apply_gravity` (`src/retrieval/gravity.rs:9`)
adds `gravity_weight * max_over_gravitons(mass * max(0, cos(entity,
graviton_vec)))` to each result's score. Default `gravity_weight` is `0.15`
(`src/config/retrieval.rs:82`); setting it to `0` returns immediately at zero
cost, and a graph with no gravitons is a no-op. The combination is a **max, not
a sum**, so two overlapping gravitons over the same subject matter cannot
double-count a result into the top of the list.

Because heat governs retention and gravitons govern placement and ranking, they
compound without being wired together: material that lands in a well-populated
graviton's subtree sits closer to the root, is reached by the pulse at higher
strength, is reinforced more often, and consequently survives GC more readily.
Aiming a graviton at a subject is an indirect but real statement about what the
graph should keep.

### Auto-promotion out of `generic`

`generic` is not a dumping ground with no exit. When the tick names a kern
(`do_name`, `src/tick/tasks.rs:224`) it immediately calls
`promote_to_root_if_generic` (`src/base/accept.rs:671`). If the freshly named
kern's parent is `generic`, and no existing root graviton is equivalent to its
new name or vector, it is detached from `generic` and reparented to the root —
becoming a graviton in its own right. A dense enough cluster of unfiled material
promotes itself into a focus, without anyone declaring the topic in advance.

## Why plural, not singular

Gravitons replaced a single per-kern `purpose` field on 2026-07-19
(`docs/oracle/CHANGELOG.md`). The rename touched roughly 280 sites across types,
routing, the MCP tool, the CLI, gossip and the digest — but the substance of the
change is not the name, it is the arity.

One purpose per kern forces a single answer to "what is this memory about", and
every real memory has several. It made three things impossible that the plural
model makes routine:

- **Overlapping focuses.** A graph can be simultaneously about a codebase, an
  ongoing incident, and a person, and a new item can be scored against all three
  rather than being forced into whichever one happened to be the kern's declared
  purpose.
- **Weighting.** With one purpose there is nothing to weigh it against, so
  `mass` has no meaning. With several, mass is how you say *this focus matters
  more right now* — heavier gravitons both attract at ingest and pull harder at
  query time — without deleting the others.
- **Query-time bias at all.** A single purpose was effectively a routing label.
  A set of gravitons is a vector field over the graph, which is what makes the
  `gravity.rs` ranking pass expressible as one bounded pass over the results.

Two costs were named alongside it. The gossip wire fields renamed from
`anchor_*` to `graviton_*`, which breaks federation peers that predate the
change — accepted, as federation is opt-in LAN and pre-1.0. And the query-time
pass is not free: measured on a workload trace over three-run medians, recall@10
and NDCG@10 were unchanged with gravity on or off, at roughly **+7% p50 latency
with five gravitons** and zero cost with none. Gravity is a bias knob, not a
recall improvement, and the benchmark says so.

## Observing and forcing it

The sweep is autonomous, but it is not opaque. The `health` MCP tool reports
graviton, kern, entity and reason counts, which is the fastest way to see
whether unnamed-kern churn is accumulating. The `gc` tool (`tool_gc`,
`src/mcp/tools_admin.rs:178`) is a *different* collector from the one on this
page — it reaps empty and orphan kerns from the live graph and persists, rather
than evicting cold entities — and it is the right thing to reach for when the
structure, not the content, has grown residue.

One caveat it reports itself: deleting rows frees pages inside the LMDB file but
does not return that disk to the operating system. The file shrinks on the next
restart, which auto-compacts, or via an offline `kern compact`. A `gc` that
reports thousands of reaped kerns and an unchanged `data.mdb` size is behaving
correctly.
